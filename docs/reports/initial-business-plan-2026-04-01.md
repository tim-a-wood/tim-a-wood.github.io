# Initial Business Plan — MV Metroidvania Toolchain

**Date:** April 1, 2026  
**Prepared by:** Strategy (with Finance, Marketing, and Legal lenses)  
**Audience:** Founder  

This report is for planning. It is not tax, legal, or investment advice. Numbers are illustrations with stated assumptions, not promises.

---

## What we are building

We run two things that help each other:

1. **The Workbench** — browser tools for sprites, rooms, and world layout, with AI-assisted help where it fits.  
2. **The game** — a metroidvania-style game (for example *Ashen Hollow*) built with those tools.

The game proves the tools. The tools make the next games faster to build. That loop is our main long-term advantage.

We also want **several small software products** over time — apps, sites, utilities, and games — so income does not depend on one launch.

---

## Who we serve

**Now:** Indie and solo developers making 2D platformers and metroidvanias. They care about honest demos, not hype.

**Later:** The same people when they need reliability, docs, and clear pricing — after we have earned trust with early adopters.

---

## How we make money (direction)

**Workbench**

- Plan toward a **free core** with a **paid tier** for heavier AI use, once the AI features are clearly strong enough to justify the price.  
- **Important money rule:** What we charge per user must stay **above** what we pay for AI and hosting for that user. If not, selling more makes us poorer.

**Game**

- Sales on **itch.io** and **Steam** (when ready), plus possible add-ons later (soundtrack, extras).

**Side products**

- Small paid assets, templates, micro-tools, or simple subscriptions. These should be cheap to maintain after the first build.

---

## Other income ideas (low upkeep where possible)

These still need occasional updates and support; “passive” really means **low touch**, not zero work.

- **Asset packs and templates** sold on itch or similar.  
- **Tiny web utilities** (tips, ads, or one-time purchase) — keep privacy simple.  
- **Teaching or a paid devlog tier** — only promise what we can deliver.  
- **Affiliate links** in honest gear or tool lists — always say when we earn a fee.  
- **Engine plugins** later, once export formats are stable — good for stickiness, not a day-one cash grab.

---

## Market snapshot (big picture)

The **indie game** space and **AI-assisted creation tools** are both growing in public reports. Exact numbers differ by source; treat them as **directional**, not precise.

Trends that matter for us:

- More developers are trying AI in their workflow.  
- Trust is still shaky — people want **human control** and clear labeling when AI is involved.  
- Specialized tools that chain together are common; an **integrated** sprite-plus-room pipeline is still relatively rare.

## Money scenarios (illustrative only)

These bands assume you eventually sell a **paid Workbench tier**, a **priced game**, and some **small side products**. Real results depend on audience size, conversion, and costs.

| Year | Lower band | Middle band | Upper band |
|------|------------|-------------|------------|
| 1 | Roughly none to a few thousand dollars | About five to twenty-five thousand | Up to low six figures if a launch hits |
| 2 | A few thousand to mid-teens | About thirty to ninety thousand | Into low hundreds of thousands if the tool and game both land |
| 3 | About ten to forty thousand | About eighty to two hundred thousand | Much higher only with strong retention and controlled costs |

**What moves the numbers most:** how many people pay for the tool, how much AI each person uses, and whether the game finds an audience.

**Costs to watch:** AI API bills, storefront cuts, and your own time (which has a real opportunity cost).

---

## Quick wins (next three months)

1. **Ship things people can share** — short clips or exports that show the workflow.  
2. **Put the game where people buy indie games** — start with itch; plan Steam when it is ready.  
3. **Launch one small paid item** — for example a template or asset pack — to learn pricing and fees.  
4. **Track AI cost per session** before advertising “unlimited AI.”  
5. **Before taking payment or emails at scale:** clear **terms**, **privacy**, and **AI disclosure** text; have a real lawyer review when it becomes material.

---

## Legal and trust (summary)

- Outputs that are **mostly AI** may get **weak copyright** protection; **human choices** (edits, selection, layout) matter for IP stories.  
- Label AI-generated suggestions clearly in the product.  
- Read cloud AI **terms** before bundling their outputs into another paid API or white-label service.  
- For anything that creates real obligations (contracts, full ToS launch), use a **qualified attorney**.

---

## Risks (honest list)

- AI features cost money; bad pricing or heavy use can **erase profit**.  
- Too many side projects can **delay** the game and Workbench.  
- Overpromising AI **hurts trust** in indie communities.  
- Dependence on **one store** or **one API** is fragile.  
- Legal rules around AI and data keep **evolving**.

---

## Recommendation

Keep the **game plus Workbench** as the main story. Fund a **small, fixed budget** of experiments (two to four at a time). Every ninety days, **keep or drop** each experiment based on simple numbers and time spent. Start charging for the Workbench only when the paid AI experience is **clearly worth it** compared to alternatives. Until then, lean on **game sales, assets, and education** where the economics are easier to control.

---

## Risks

Same as the list above; the largest operational risk is **AI cost versus price**, and the largest strategic risk is **split focus**.
