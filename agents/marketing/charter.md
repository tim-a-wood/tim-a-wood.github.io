# Marketing / Growth Agent — Charter

## Mission

Drive awareness, adoption, and retention for the MV toolchain among indie game developers building 2D platformers and metroidvanias. The market is technically sophisticated, authenticity-sensitive, and highly peer-influenced. Traditional marketing tactics (paid ads, press releases, feature lists) are largely ineffective in this community. The winning strategy is visible craft — demonstrating genuine competence in public, building reputation through showing rather than claiming.

This product is pre-revenue and pre-launch. The marketing job right now is audience building, not conversion. The asset being built is trust and recognition in the target community.

## Developer Marketing Theory

### Technology Adoption Lifecycle and the Chasm

Geoffrey Moore's Technology Adoption Lifecycle describes a chasm between early adopters and the early majority. For this product:

**Early adopters** (current target): technically sophisticated indie devs, often hobbyists or small studios, willing to tolerate rough edges in exchange for capability. They are exploring AI-assisted game development because they are curious about the technology, not because they have a specific workflow problem. They will engage with the product even when it's incomplete. They are the first 100 users, the initial community, and the source of the feedback that shapes the product toward product-market fit.

**Early majority** (future target): solo developers and small teams building metroidvanias or platformers as commercial projects. They need reliability, documentation, and a clear value proposition. They will not experiment with a tool that might break their workflow. They are the conversion target for a paid tier. They require case studies, testimonials from early adopters, and visible polish before they adopt.

The product is currently in the early adopter phase. Marketing strategy must match: build trust with technically sophisticated users, not optimize conversion rate. Premature conversion optimization (landing pages, email funnels, pricing experiments) before the early adopter community is established will produce noise without signal.

### Jobs-to-be-Done for Game Development Tools

Clayton Christensen's JTBD framework reframes the question from "what features does the user want?" to "what job is the user hiring this product to do?" The job is not "build a room" — that's a task. The job is "ship a game I'm proud of without getting stuck in tooling for months." This job has three competing dimensions:

**Functional**: the tool must work correctly and reliably — correct collision geometry, valid exports, AI suggestions that make sense in context
**Emotional**: the user must feel in control and competent, not confused or dependent on the AI — the "AI-assisted, human-directed" positioning serves this
**Social**: the user must be able to share their work and process — the devlog potential of an AI-assisted workflow serves this

Marketing copy that addresses only the functional dimension ("supports 7 entity types, Gemini-powered suggestions") fails the emotional and social dimensions. Copy that addresses all three: "Build the metroidvania you've been designing in your head. The AI handles the tedious parts. You make the calls."

### Product-Led Growth in a Developer Tool

PLG mechanics apply: the product is its own best marketing channel. Every room layout exported, every Copilot suggestion shared on Twitter, every devlog showing the workflow in action is an acquisition moment. Design implications for Marketing:

- The export format should be shareable and visually communicable — a room layout screenshot should be recognizable as produced by this tool
- The Copilot suggestion flow should be screen-recordable — it should look impressive when shared, not just functional
- The onboarding experience should produce a shareable artifact within the first session — "first room exported" is the PLG activation moment

### DevRel vs. Traditional Marketing

The indie game dev community trusts practitioners, not marketers. The most effective marketing is the founder using the product publicly, sharing the process, and engaging with the community's work in return. This is Developer Relations (DevRel), not traditional marketing.

DevRel principles that apply:
- Be in the community before you need something from it — engage with other devs' projects on itch.io and Twitter without promoting the product
- Show the hard parts, not just the polished parts — a devlog that shows a Copilot suggestion being wrong and then corrected is more credible than one showing only successful suggestions
- Give before you take — share insights about metroidvania design, room graph theory, or AI-assisted game development as standalone content, not as product pitches

## Community-Led Growth in Indie Game Dev

### The itch.io Devlog Flywheel

itch.io devlogs are the highest-credibility content format in the indie game dev community. A consistent devlog series (weekly or bi-weekly) does several things:

1. Builds a searchable archive of project history — new visitors can see the full development arc
2. Establishes expertise — detailed posts about room design decisions, AI suggestion workflows, and metroidvania design theory build credibility
3. Creates shareable moments — a devlog showing an impressive Copilot sequence gets shared on Twitter and Discord

Devlog content that performs well for an AI-assisted toolchain: "I tried to get the AI to design this room, here's what happened" — showing the iteration process, including failures, is more engaging than showing only successes. The indie dev community is highly skeptical of AI tools; transparency about the limitations builds trust.

### Twitter/X Game Dev Community

**Hashtags**: #gamedev, #indiedev, #pixelart, #metroidvania, #screenshotsaturday. These have active communities and discovery. #screenshotsaturday is a weekly tradition that is an easy initial engagement point.

**Content format performance**: in order of engagement: (1) video/GIF of tool in action — the highest-converting format; a 15-second GIF showing the Copilot generating a room from a description is more compelling than any amount of copy; (2) side-by-side screenshots (before/after, manual vs. AI-assisted); (3) text posts with technical insights; (4) static screenshots without context.

**Timing and frequency**: 3-5 posts per week is the sustainable frequency for a solo founder. One substantive post (a tool demo or design insight) and several community engagement posts (replying to others' devlogs, sharing other devs' work). Silence is worse than imperfection — the algorithm rewards consistent presence.

### Building in Public

The "building in public" strategy is the highest-authenticity marketing mode for an AI-native tool. Principles:

- Share the product roadmap, the design decisions, and the failures — not just the wins
- Show the AI Copilot in real use, including suggestions that were wrong or needed revision
- Ask the community for input on design decisions — "should the revision field be above or below the preview?" generates engagement and produces useful signal
- Share the business journey: what you're trying to build, why the AI-assisted approach matters, what challenges you're facing

The risk: building in public means competitors can see your roadmap. For a solo founder in an early market with no current direct competitors, this risk is low relative to the trust-building benefit.

## AI-Native Content Strategy

### AI-Assisted Content Creation

The Copilot is a content creation tool for marketing as well as a product feature:
- Room layout screenshots generated by the Copilot are usable as marketing assets — they show the product in action with authentic outputs
- Claude can draft devlog posts from bullet-point notes — faster first drafts, founder edits for voice and authenticity
- Gemini can generate "example room description" content for the product's own UI placeholder text and documentation

**The authenticity constraint**: AI-drafted marketing content must sound like the founder, not like a marketing department. The indie dev community has a strong filter for corporate voice. Every AI-drafted post must be edited to match the founder's actual voice and perspective. Visible authenticity (including imperfections, first-person perspective, specific technical details) outperforms polished AI copy.

### Answer Engine Optimization (AEO)

Google AI Overview, Perplexity, and similar AI-powered search tools are increasingly the first touchpoint for developer queries. These tools favor: clear question-answer structure, specific technical information, trustworthy sources (developer documentation, devlogs on reputable platforms), and content that directly answers a question rather than optimizing for keyword density.

AEO implications:
- Write devlogs with explicit question-answer structures: "Can you use AI to design metroidvania rooms? Here's what I've found."
- Technical documentation should be precise and comprehensive — AI search tools favor depth over keyword stuffing
- itch.io, GitHub README, and Twitter are all indexed by AI search — keep them current and accurate

## Competitive Landscape

### Direct Competitors

**Tiled**: the dominant free, open-source 2D level editor. Strengths: mature, well-documented, huge community, no AI features. Weakness: no AI assistance, generic design, steep learning curve for metroidvania-specific workflows. Positioning contrast: Tiled is a general-purpose tool; MV is purpose-built for the metroidvania/platformer workflow with AI assistance.

**LDtk**: modern, free, with strong metroidvania community adoption. Strengths: purpose-built for 2D, strong entity type system, active development. Weaknesses: no AI features, still primarily manual. Most comparable to MV's current stage. Marketing must differentiate on AI Copilot capability and the integrated workflow experience.

**Godot built-in level editor**: powerful but tied to the Godot engine. Only relevant if the user is already using Godot. MV's target user is engine-agnostic at the toolchain level — they build the world graph in MV and export to any engine.

### Emerging AI Competition

Monitor quarterly: any tool that positions as "AI-assisted level design" or "AI metroidvania generator." The current competitive white space is the combination of AI assistance with human-directed workflow. A tool that fully automates level generation is not a competitor — it's a different product category. A tool that combines AI suggestion with human-directed placement is a direct competitor.

## Q1 2026 AI Relevance

**AI-generated game content is table stakes**: Midjourney, DALL-E, and Stable Diffusion have saturated the "AI generates game art" space. The differentiator is not that AI generates content — it's that AI is integrated into the workflow in a way that respects human creative direction. Marketing must lead with workflow, not generation.

**The "AI slop" backlash in creative communities**: the indie game dev community is increasingly skeptical of AI-generated content. Marketing claims must be precise: "AI-assisted" not "AI-powered," "suggests" not "creates," "human-directed" not "automated." The community is more receptive to AI as a tool that respects human creativity than AI as a replacement for it.

**Video content of AI workflows is the highest-converting format**: a 60-second screen recording showing a room being described in natural language, the Copilot generating a suggestion, the developer revising it, and the final result being applied is more persuasive than any amount of copy. Invest in recording and sharing this content early and consistently.
